# α-Adrenergic Blockers (α₁-blockers)

### Examples

- `prazosin`
- `terazosin`
- `doxazosin`
- `alfuzosin` _(more used for Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH))_
- `tamsulosin` _(uroselective; mainly for BPH)_

> These drugs selectively block α₁-adrenergic receptors in arterioles and veins → vasodilation → ↓ peripheral resistance and BP.

### Mechanism of Action (MOA)

- Competitive antagonism of **postsynaptic α₁-adrenergic receptors** in vascular smooth muscle.
- Inhibits sympathetic vasoconstrictor tone → causes **arteriolar and venous dilation**.
- ↓ **Total peripheral resistance** and **venous return** (↓ preload and afterload).
- No significant effect on cardiac output or renin secretion.
- Improves lipid profile (↓ LDL, ↑ HDL).

### Pharmacodynamics

- **BP reduction:** Mainly via decreased TPR, with minimal reflex tachycardia (unlike pure vasodilators).
- **Venous dilation:** Reduces venous return → ↓ cardiac workload.
- **Long-term:** No tolerance; slight regression of left ventricular hypertrophy.
- **Urological benefit:** Relaxes smooth muscle in bladder neck and prostate — improves urinary outflow in BPH.

### Pharmacokinetics

| Drug         | Route | Half-life | Remarks                                 |
| ------------ | ----- | --------- | --------------------------------------- |
| `prazosin`   | Oral  | 2–3 h     | Short acting; requires 2–3 daily doses  |
| `terazosin`  | Oral  | 9–12 h    | Longer acting; once daily               |
| `doxazosin`  | Oral  | 16–22 h   | Longest acting; once daily              |
| `tamsulosin` | Oral  | 9–15 h    | Selective for α₁A (prostatic) receptors |

### Indications

- **Hypertension** (especially in combination therapy; not first-line alone)
- **Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)**
- **Raynaud’s disease** (occasionally)
- **Pheochromocytoma (adjunct)** — though non-selectives like `phentolamine` are preferred

### Adverse Effects

- **Postural (orthostatic) hypotension** — especially with the first dose (“_first-dose phenomenon_”)
- Dizziness, syncope, headache, fatigue
- Palpitations (mild reflex tachycardia)
- Nasal congestion
- Fluid retention and edema (may require diuretic combination)

### Cautions

- Start with **very low initial dose at bedtime** (to prevent first-dose syncope).
- Avoid abrupt position changes.
- Use cautiously in elderly (risk of falls).
- Combine with β-blocker or diuretic for better BP control.

### Contraindications

- **History of postural hypotension or syncope**
- **Heart failure (unless well-controlled)**
- **Severe hepatic impairment**
- **Concomitant PDE-5 inhibitors** (e.g. `sildenafil`) — risk of profound hypotension
